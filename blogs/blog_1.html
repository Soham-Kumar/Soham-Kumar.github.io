How to View Your Life

Published: November 20, 2024 Tags: PhilosophySelf-Reflection

भूमिका

Venturing into life, especially at the stage where one becomes both capable and compelled to make decisions, presents two paths: the well-defined path usually shaped by societal norms, or the path of approaching life with first principles. While first-principle thinking may clash with practical realities, and much of our future is influenced by past decisions, to be truly conscious is to strive to define one's life's foundational axioms and let actions stem from them, rather than being solely dictated by the weight of what has been.1 2 This essay explores one such potential view of life, born from grappling with its inherent meaninglessness and potential emptiness.

The Shadow of Meaninglessness

"Man is condemned to be free. Condemned, because he did not create himself; yet in other respects is free because, once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does."

Jean-Paul Sartre, Existentialism and Human Emotions

Sartre captures the human condition beautifully: we are thrown into existence without choosing it, yet we are inescapably free, burdened with the responsibility of forging meaning in a universe that offers none intrinsically. Humanity exists between animals driven purely by instinct and hypothetical, unconstrained gods. This freedom compels reflection; it necessitates finding a raison d'être to navigate the inherent sufferings and challenges of existence.3

Nature, optimized for self-perpetuation, not necessarily individual fulfillment, provides "trinkets" – fleeting pleasures, biological drives – to keep the machinery running. These might suffice for a time, but often, a point arrives where these motivations feel hollow. This realization can manifest as an undercurrent of deep, unwavering meaninglessness – or, to be more precise, a strong sense of निस्सारता. This feeling permeates both good times and the bad, a persistent sense that the work one does is inconsequential, that nothing truly worthwhile will ever emerge.

This feeling isn't just abstract; it can become intensely personal. For instance, I recall feeling lonely in college near the semester's end, when homesickness hits hard, when the longing to return to home becomes unbearable. But after returning home several times, this comfort that I sought in home, too faded; home lost its refuge status. The mental sanctuary, the place where everything would supposedly be okay, dissolved. The sleepless nights studying felt more hollow, the ups and downs stirred me – the feeling of निस्सारता dominated it all, like a deep, endless abyss. As one grows, he loses his ramparts and the things he associated with comfort, a phenomenon Freud noted leads to a specific kind of melancholy.4 This experience, where even anticipated comforts failed, underscored the pervasiveness of this potential void, pushing the existential questioning highlighted by thinkers like Camus from the abstract into lived reality. It suggests that meaning isn't simply found in external comforts or familiar places.

But why this inherent lack of external meaning? Perhaps asking for "the meaning of life" is like asking for a linear answer to a circular question, as illustrated by the following dialogue:

Person A: Why do you exist?

Person B: ...well, I exist because my parents reproduced.

Person A: That doesn't explain why your parents exist. I could keep asking 'why' back to the Big Bang. Then you'd say 'because existence began,' and I'd ask 'why did existence begin?' We're back where we started. So, why you?

Person B: Uhm... I exist to bring future children into the world?

Person A: Same problem, different direction. I can ask 'why' until the heat death of the universe. You run out of answers. There's no ultimate external 'why'. Looking backward, it's contingency; looking forward, it ends. Does that mean it's all for nothing?

Person A: What's it ALL for? If the answer refers to something else (like 'heaven'), that 'something else' immediately becomes part of the 'ALL' we're asking about. The question loops back onto itself.

(Dialogue adapted from this video source)

If life cannot derive meaning from something entirely outside itself, as the dialogue suggests, perhaps the meaning is intrinsic: the meaning of life is that it is, that it exists and one experiences it. But, as the feeling of निस्सारता demonstrates, experience alone often feels insufficient against this weight.

Life as a Mission to Evade Pettiness

Given that neither external cosmic purpose nor simple experience reliably counters this निस्सारता, what remains? Perhaps a conscious mission: the evasion of pettiness. Pettiness here means dancing to the tune of those same biological or societal trinkets – security over aliveness, money over passion and freedom, etc. Adhering to these trinkets and deriving fun from them is the path of least resistance, often marked by a lack of deep, meaningful struggle. As Dinkar writes:

युद्ध नहीं जिनके जीवन में वे भी बहुत अभागे होंगे,
या तो प्रण को तोड़ा होगा या फिर रण से भागे होंगे।

दिनकर

A life devoid of "युद्ध" – a struggle for something meaningful – implies a compromise, an evasion of challenges that could lend substance to life, a point echoed by Wilhelm Reich:

“You differ from a great man in only one respect: the great man was once a very little man, but he developed one important quality: he recognized the smallness and narrowness of his thoughts and actions. Under the pressure of some task that meant a great deal to him, he learned to see how his smallness, his pettiness endangered his happiness. In other words, a great man knows when and in what way he is a little man. A little man does not know he is little and is afraid to know. He hides his pettiness and narrowness behind illusions of strength and greatness, someone else's strength and greatness. He's proud of his great generals but not of himself. He admires an idea he has not had, not one he has had. The less he understands something, the more firmly he believes in it. And the better he understands an idea, the less he believes in it.”

Wilhelm Reich, Listen, Little Man!

Reich's words powerfully illustrate the nature of this pettiness – a state characterized by reliance on external validation and avoidance of self-awareness – which he argues actively undermines genuine happiness.

So, if the goal is to evade this state, how is this achieved in practice? Evolution provides a clue: humans distinguished themselves by creating value – fire, wheels, poetry, paintings. This act of creation birthed not only comfort and distinction but also conscience – the capacity to judge our own actions, the watcher (दृष्टा) observing the doer (कर्ता). This boon (or bane) compels us to form a self-perception based on our choices (बीज and कूटस्थ) – this self opinion stays with one for all the time he's awake, and for the time he's dreaming. Consequently, to live is to either live with an abysmal opinion of yourself – a melancholic existence, often masked by consumerist trinkets – or to make choices that cultivate a rich self-perception. We are constantly in our own company; this company becomes intolerable if we perceive ourselves as degraded.

Pettiness isn’t inherently bad because it opposes some abstract ideal of 'greatness,' but because living steeped in it feels melancholic – not a dramatic despair, but a constant, biting, menacing sadness that irritates without necessarily provoking revolt. The antidote, therefore, lies in consciously choosing activities that build a positive self-perception, specifically those that pull us away from the allure of easy comforts towards the rabbit hole of value creation.

What, then, constitutes these 'value-creating' activities? They generally share these characteristics:

  1. They are often intrinsically engaging, even 'fun' in the sense of requiring significant effort and cognitive load, resulting in something valuable (to oneself, at least).
  2. While the initial impetus might be external (e.g., studying for an exam), genuine engagement involves going beyond necessity, exploring depths not required for the immediate goal, transforming obligation into interest.
  3. They are difficult, posing challenges to current skills and demanding focus.5

Crucially, the 'value' created isn't always practical or utilitarian. It resides in the effort, the objective (sometimes implicit), and the beauty perceived in the outcome or the process. Value leads to beauty, and beauty reinforces value. These pursuits provide inherent satisfaction, making one's own company bearable precisely because they counter the feeling of self-degradation associated with pettiness.

Life as Creative Endeavor (सृजनात्मकता)

This very act of evading pettiness through value creation finds its most positive expression when embraced as a commitment to सृजनात्मकता, or creativity. This isn't just about avoiding the negative; it's about actively participating in the act of bringing something new or meaningful into being. The feeling of निस्सारता finds its counterpoint in the चाह सृजन की – the longing for creation. As Agyeya powerfully illustrates in "उड़ चल, हारिल":

उड़ चल, हारिल, लिये हाथ में यही अकेला ओछा तिनका।
ऊषा जाग उठी प्राची में-कैसी बाट, भरोसा किन का!
शक्ति रहे तेरे हाथों में-छुट न जाय यह चाह सृजन की;
शक्ति रहे तेरे हाथों में-रुक न जाय यह गति जीवन की!

...

तिनका? तेरे हाथों में है अमर एक रचना का साधन-
तिनका? तेरे पंजे में है विधना के प्राणों का स्पन्दन!

...

मिट्टी निश्चय है यथार्थ, पर क्या जीवन केवल मिट्टी है?
तू मिट्टी, पर मिट्टी से उठने की इच्छा किस ने दी है?
आज उसी ऊध्र्वंग ज्वाल का तू है दुर्निवार हरकारा

...

उड़ चल हारिल, लिये हाथ में एक अकेला पावन तिनका!

अज्ञेय

Agyeya's Hornbill, clutching a mere straw ("ओछा तिनका"), becomes a symbol for the human spirit compelled to create, to fly upwards despite starting from शून्य नभ – the intrinsically meaningless life. The poem affirms that the creative impulse, "चाह सृजन की", emanating from the purposeless (निस्सार) मिट्टी itself, connects us to something potentially अमर (immortal/timeless) through रचना (creation). Life, viewed this way, becomes an answer to this internal call, using whatever 'straw' we possess to participate in creation, however small, making it a bulwark against the menacing meaninglessness.

The Resonant Peaks: Where Creation Meets Understanding

So, if life becomes this determined evasion of pettiness, this relentless drive towards सृजनात्मकता, where does this path ultimately lead? What peak experiences affirm this choice, offering not just distraction from निस्सारता, but a profound, resonant meaning? Having committed to the struggle, wielding our 'single humble straw' against the void, we find certain experiences offer the clearest validation of the strength of सृजनात्मकता in the face of perplexing meaninglessness. These are not mere entertainments – they are deeds that satisfy the deepest intellectual and existential needs of a conscious life engaged in this creative struggle.

For me, two such peaks stand out, representing perhaps the most profound ways the human spirit engages with reality and finds beauty potent enough to counter meaninglessness. They embody the twin powers cultivated by this striving: the power to find order within the seeming chaos of existence, and the power to appreciate and impose intricate order upon it.

The first is the revelatory power found in art, perhaps most purely in poetry (and prose that achieves a similar density of meaning). After wrestling with the confusing, often painful, chaotic flow of life, one may stumble upon words that perfectly encapsulate a hard-won, nebulous understanding gained from living through those tumultuous times. It’s the magic of abstraction hitting home – where the bewildering randomness of personal experience is suddenly mirrored, articulated, and given form by language, distilled into a truth that resonates deep within. It’s like finding an unexpected map for the territories you've already traversed blindly, discovering that the tangled paths can make sense. Here, the struggle against meaninglessness finds resonance; chaos yields to understanding, and lived experience finds its articulate form. This beauty emerges as order articulated from the raw material of life itself.

The second is the exhilarating clarity derived from understanding complex, ordered systems. This is the deep satisfaction found in grasping the architecture of an elegant solution to a complex problem in engineering, science, or mathematics. It involves understanding an intricate problem, planning a response, making sense of the interplay of components (often built upon layers of abstraction), and witnessing how they combine – sometimes counter-intuitively – to produce a functional, often beautiful, whole from potentially chaotic inputs.6 There are moments of pure intellectual ecstasy here – appreciating the problem's daunting complexity, the ingenuity of the solution's synthesis, and the almost magical efficacy of the result. Here, the creative impulse imposes sense upon complexity; intellect finds elegant order. This beauty arises from human reason structuring reality, providing an unimaginable joy that directly counters the feeling of futility.

The ascent to either peak requires effort. Patiently learning the foundational tools – the grammar of language and its difficult metaphors, the rigorous theory of mathematics and science, the context of philosophy – demands struggle and often feels removed from the promised vista. Yet, these very efforts build the capacity for those profound moments of comprehension, glimpses of coherence in a potentially indifferent universe. These experiences are where the mind touches something fundamental (आधारभूत), proving the value of the struggle against pettiness and, consequently, validating life itself.

Therefore, in facing the void, the path lies not in awaiting an external decree of meaning, but in the active, ongoing creation of it. This involves consciously choosing the difficult path away from pettiness, embracing the creative spark (the चाह सृजन की), and crucially, seeking and cultivating the capacity to appreciate these profound forms of beauty – the beauty of chaos understood, and the beauty of order achieved. These become the tangible rewards for answering life's existential challenge with creative action.
